# VIPER Rules: Bug Report Writing

These guidelines ensure all vulnerability reports generated by VIPER are clear, concise, accurate, and professional, adhering to industry best practices and bug bounty platform requirements.

## 1. Clarity and Conciseness

### 1.1 Be Direct
- **Rule**: Start with the most critical information (vulnerability type, impact) in the executive summary.
- **Action**: Avoid jargon where plain language suffices. Explain technical terms if necessary.

### 1.2 Structured Sections
- **Rule**: Use clear headings and bullet points for readability. Follow the standard report structure: Summary, Details, Reproduction, Impact, Recommendation.
- **Action**: Automate report generation to adhere to a consistent template.

### 1.3 Minimalist Language
- **Rule**: Avoid verbose descriptions. Get straight to the point without sacrificing necessary detail.
- **Action**: AI-driven summarization and phrasing optimization during report generation.

## 2. Accuracy and Detail

### 2.1 Precise Vulnerability Naming
- **Rule**: Use industry-standard vulnerability names (e.g., "CORS Misconfiguration with Wildcard Origin" instead of "CORS Bug").
- **Action**: Map detected vulnerability patterns to known CWEs and standardized names.

### 2.2 Detailed Reproduction Steps
- **Rule**: Provide clear, step-by-step instructions for reproduction. Assume the reader has basic technical knowledge but no prior context of the vulnerability.
- **Requirement**: Each step must be explicit and repeatable.
- **Action**: For complex exploits, include exact requests (cURL, Burp Suite logs), responses, and all necessary parameters.

### 2.3 Comprehensive Evidence
- **Rule**: Include high-quality, relevant evidence that proves the vulnerability and its impact.
- **Requirements**: Screenshots (redacted), traffic logs (Burp, ZAP), PoC code snippets, video recordings (if beneficial).
- **Action**: Automatically capture and organize evidence during discovery and verification phases.

## 3. CVSS Scoring and Impact Assessment

### 3.1 Accurate CVSS Scoring
- **Rule**: Use the Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS) to objectively rate the severity of the vulnerability.
- **Requirement**: Always use the latest stable CVSS version (currently 3.1 or 4.0 if specified by program).
- **Action**: Automate CVSS vector generation and score calculation based on predefined logic and AI analysis of impact factors (Attack Vector, Attack Complexity, Privileges Required, User Interaction, Scope, Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability).

### 3.2 Clear Business Impact
- **Rule**: Translate technical impact into business risk. Explain *why* the vulnerability matters to the organization.
- **Examples**: "Leads to unauthorized data exposure impacting customer trust and GDPR compliance," "Allows unauthenticated users to modify critical business data."
- **Action**: AI models are trained on real-world impact scenarios to generate relevant business context.

### 3.3 Prioritization
- **Rule**: Provide a clear recommendation on the priority of remediation (e.g., Critical, High, Medium, Low).
- **Action**: Link prioritization directly to CVSS score and business impact.

## 4. Remediation Recommendations

### 4.1 Specific and Actionable
- **Rule**: Offer clear, actionable recommendations for fixing the vulnerability. Avoid vague suggestions.
- **Action**: Provide code-level or configuration-level guidance where appropriate. Link to official documentation or best practices.

### 4.2 Best Practices
- **Rule**: Suggest general security best practices to prevent similar vulnerabilities in the future (e.g., input validation, output encoding, least privilege principle, secure defaults).

## 5. Disclosure Etiquette

### 5.1 Program Policy Adherence
- **Rule**: Strictly follow the disclosure policy of the specific bug bounty program (e.g., public disclosure timelines, private disclosure first).
- **Action**: The submission skill (`skills/submission/SKILL.md`) is configured to respect program policies.

### 5.2 Professional Tone
- **Rule**: Maintain a professional, respectful, and collaborative tone throughout the report. Avoid aggressive or demanding language.
- **Action**: AI language models ensure reports are drafted with a diplomatic and constructive tone.

### 5.3 Researcher Identity
- **Rule**: Clearly identify the researcher (viper-ashborn) and the tools used (VIPER) where required.
- **Action**: Include a standard disclaimer about automated testing and ethical conduct.

## 6. Formatting for Platforms

### 6.1 HackerOne Specifics
- **Rule**: Adapt to HackerOne's rich text editor capabilities and required fields (e.g., Asset, Weakness, Severity).
- **Action**: Reports are generated in a HackerOne-friendly Markdown format.

### 6.2 Yogosha Specifics
- **Rule**: Align with Yogosha's reporting interface, including their specific categorization and impact fields.
- **Action**: Reports are tailored for Yogosha's platform requirements.

## 7. Versioning and Audit Trail

### 7.1 Report Versioning
- **Rule**: Each report generated, especially for a resubmission or update, should have a clear version indicator.
- **Action**: Reports are timestamped and versioned in the `reports/` directory.

### 7.2 Audit Logs
- **Rule**: All report generation and submission activities are logged for auditability.
- **Action**: Integrated with `state/submission_tracker.json` to maintain a full history.

*Effective reporting is key to successful bug bounty hunting and faster remediation cycles.*
